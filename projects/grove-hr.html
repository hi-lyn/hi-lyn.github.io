Building a GroveHR Design System

Revamping a multinational design system involves cataloging components with attention to both big picture and details. Here's my approach in one system.

Project Overview

The best HR software for startups and SMB companies to automate onboarding, performance management, time-off, attendance tracking, payroll assistance and more, with a single software.

Background

GroveHR, designed for startups and SMBs by Grove HR Company, is a comprehensive HR solution. Streamlining tasks from recruitment to analytics, the app prioritizes user-centric design. Managed by the Visual Design team, its design system ensures consistency, making GroveHR an essential, innovative tool for HR professionals worldwide.

My Role

  • Visual Design: Designing and fine-tuning interface elements to optimize the user experience.

  • Design System Management and Documentation: Creating and maintaining clear standards and guidelines to help people understand how to use the system.

Tools

Figma — where we designed all component and pattern libraries and built prototypes to demonstrate interactive elements.

The Challenge

  • Varied design elements across market websites and micro-sites create user experience disparities.

  • Conflicting design directions and guidelines lead to ambiguity in the overall design language.

  • Duplicate efforts in recreating common elements result in inefficiencies and wasted resources.

  • The urgency to deliver solutions quickly, often without thorough discovery and research, poses a challenge to quality.

  • Siloed teams, working in isolation with disjointed communication, hinder collaborative and cohesive design efforts within GroveHR.

Business Problem

Our core objective is to overcome fragmented workflows and inconsistent global brand experiences resulting from piecemeal website technology and design approaches. We aim to establish a unified brand experience, streamline workflows through integrated technology and design, and optimize user interactions. Efficiency is key, with a focus on resource utilization and accelerated time-to-market through synchronized updates. Our strategic goals aim to transform our digital landscape into a cohesive and efficient environment.

Goal

As the Grove HR team, our objective is to collaboratively develop and scale a versatile, accessible, and globally applicable design language. This aligns with our commitment to creating an intuitive and cohesive user experience across all facets of the Grove HR.

Getting Started

Steps I followed to create design system

Step 1 : Visiting the website and going through the flow to get a rough idea.

In this phase, I thoroughly navigated the entire website, established an account, defined my goals, and successfully completed the demonstrated process. However, encountering inconsistencies, I encountered challenges on certain pages that disrupted the flow. To address these issues, I meticulously documented all elements requiring redesign for a more seamless user experience.

Step 2 : Choosing an user flow and creating design system based on that.

I chose two user flows to evaluate. The first involves navigating from the landing page to selecting and accomplishing goals. The second entails logging in again and proceeding to purchase the premium account.

Step 3: Creating design foundation.

With all prerequisites in place, let's initiate the design foundation, encompassing colors, typography, grids, styles, shadows, and icons.

Beginning with typography, the selection of typefaces is critical. Determining the appropriate typeface, font sizes, and weights for various elements is the first consideration.

Next in line is the color scheme. It involves categorizing colors based on their usage properties. In this product, blue takes precedence as the dominant color and is designated as the primary color. Additionally, the primary color category encompasses the colors used for call-to-action buttons.


GroveHR not only contain text but also icons in different pixels.

Finally, the assets arrive. Assets are basically the images, logos, and PNGs used on websites.


Here, our design foundation comes to an end. This is all created as a component and stored in the library for further use.


Step 4: Create Components

This serves as the backbone of the design system. While creating components may seem challenging initially, it proves invaluable for designers in the long run. By establishing a single master component, adjustments to other instances can be made effortlessly by tweaking specific properties and values. If this concept feels overwhelming, let me illustrate it with an example.

The image below illustrates the creation of a component.

In this part, we need to create buttons, a navigation bar, cards, hovering texts, and much more. Take a look at the components created for this product.

Step 5: GroveHR base is ready.

Now it’s time to put it in the oven. Let’s begin redesigning the page. These are the 2 user flows I mentioned at the beginning:

Documentation

To address this challenge, we established and maintained documentation resources within a dedicated Confluence space. This collaborative effort involved close coordination with design, content author, and development partners to cater to their specific requirements. The documentation encompassed:

  1. Design Principles: Outlining the core principles guiding the design system.

  2. Quick Start Guides: Providing straightforward guides for easy system adoption.

  3. Accessibility Standards: Detailing standards to ensure the system's inclusivity.

  4. Best Practices: Offering insights into effective design and testing methodologies.

  5. Brand Governance Documents: Tailoring governance materials for each unique brand within Grove HR.

Learnings & Takeaways

  • Creating and managing design system at scale

  • Balancing pace and perfection

  • Receiving feedback and iterating
